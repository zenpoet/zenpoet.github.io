36    /**
37    * Implements the Gregorian/Julian calendar system which is the calendar system
38    * used in most of the world. Wherever possible, it is recommended to use the
39    * {@link ISOChronology} instead.
40    * <p>
41    * The Gregorian calendar replaced the Julian calendar, and the point in time
42    * when this chronology switches can be controlled using the second parameter
43    * of the getInstance method. By default this cutover is set to the date the
44    * Gregorian calendar was first instituted, October 15, 1582.
45    * <p>
46    * Before this date, this chronology uses the proleptic Julian calendar
47    * (proleptic means extending indefinitely). The Julian calendar has leap years
48    * every four years, whereas the Gregorian has special rules for 100 and 400
49    * years. A meaningful result will thus be obtained for all input values.
50    * However before 8 CE, Julian leap years were irregular, and before 45 BCE
51    * there was no Julian calendar.
52    * <p>
53    * This chronology differs from
54    * {@link java.util.GregorianCalendar GregorianCalendar} in that years
55    * in BCE are returned correctly. Thus year 1 BCE is returned as -1 instead of 1.
56    * The yearOfEra field produces results compatible with GregorianCalendar.
57    * <p>
58    * The Julian calendar does not have a year zero, and so year -1 is followed by
59    * year 1. If the Gregorian cutover date is specified at or before year -1
60    * (Julian), year zero is defined. In other words, the proleptic Gregorian
61    * chronology used by this class has a year zero.
62    * <p>
63    * To create a pure proleptic Julian chronology, use {@link JulianChronology},
64    * and to create a pure proleptic Gregorian chronology, use
65    * {@link GregorianChronology}.
66    * <p>
67    * GJChronology is thread-safe and immutable.
68    *
69    * @author Brian S O'Neill
70    * @author Stephen Colebourne
71    * @since 1.0
72    */
