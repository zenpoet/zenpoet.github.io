27    /**
28    * Implements the Islamic, or Hijri, calendar system using arithmetic rules.
29    * <p>
30    * This calendar is a lunar calendar with a shorter year than ISO.
31    * Year 1 in the Islamic calendar began on July 16, 622 CE (Julian), thus
32    * Islamic years do not begin at the same time as Julian years. This chronology
33    * is not proleptic, as it does not allow dates before the first Islamic year.
34    * <p>
35    * There are two basic forms of the Islamic calendar, the tabular and the
36    * observed. The observed form cannot easily be used by computers as it
37    * relies on human observation of the new moon.
38    * The tabular calendar, implemented here, is an arithmetical approximation
39    * of the observed form that follows relatively simple rules.
40    * <p>
41    * The tabular form of the calendar defines 12 months of alternately
42    * 30 and 29 days. The last month is extended to 30 days in a leap year.
43    * Leap years occur according to a 30 year cycle. There are four recognised
44    * patterns of leap years in the 30 year cycle:
45    * <pre>
46    * Years 2, 5, 7, 10, 13, 15, 18, 21, 24, 26 & 29 - 15-based, used by Microsoft
47    * Years 2, 5, 7, 10, 13, 16, 18, 21, 24, 26 & 29 - 16-based, most commonly used
48    * Years 2, 5, 8, 10, 13, 16, 19, 21, 24, 27 & 29 - Indian
49    * Years 2, 5, 8, 11, 13, 16, 19, 21, 24, 27 & 30 - Habash al-Hasib
50    * </pre>
51    * You can select which pattern to use via the factory methods, or use the
52    * default (16-based).
53    * <p>
54    * This implementation defines a day as midnight to midnight exactly as per
55    * the ISO chronology. This correct start of day is at sunset on the previous
56    * day, however this cannot readily be modelled and has been ignored.
57    * <p>
58    * IslamicChronology is thread-safe and immutable.
59    *
60    * @see <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_calendar">Wikipedia</a>
61    *
62    * @author Stephen Colebourne
63    * @since 1.2
64    */
